# Leyline: KV Cache Directives for Agentic Inference 

Bole Ma, Jan Eitzinger, and Harald Köstler 

Erlangen National High Performance Computing Center Erlangen, Germany 

{bole.ma, jan.eitzinger, harald.koestler}@fau.de 

Abstract 

Modern KV cache management assumes the chatbot workload: prompts arrive once and the cache grows append-only, so prefix caching and forward-only eviction are correct by construc-tion. Agentic LLMs (Yao et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) break this assumption. Their con-versations evolve through policy-driven edit-ing : failed tool calls are retried, stale outputs dropped, trajectories pivoted. Two distinct cache problems result. First, identical content moves to new positions between turns, invali-dating exact-prefix caches even though the un-derlying KV would still be valid; recent work on position-independent caching for MLA ad-dresses this reuse problem (Ma et al., 2026). Second, and this paper’s focus, a policy may need to direct the serving system to actively remove or replace a span of cached content and continue without re-prefilling everything that came after. No existing primitive offers this. Production agentic harnesses fall back to re-prefill on every edit (Anthropic, 2025), pay-ing full prefix-recomputation cost; kernel-level eviction methods (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024) make their own deci-sions and cannot accept directives from a policy outside the kernel. We introduce Leyline, a serving-side primitive that closes this gap. The key insight is that a declarative (span , replacement) 4-tuple can separate what to edit from how to preserve position correctness. The policy declares the edit and its mode (in-place splice or prefix-trimmed re-prefill for semantic forgetting); an architecture-agnostic interface routes to a per-architecture kernel that restores attention math via a closed-form RoPE-rotation correction. We validate on two legs. On the mechanism side, the splice kernel lifts replay cache-hit by 

+11 .2 pp and cuts latency by up to 241 ms by reusing prefix work the prompt edit would oth-erwise have destroyed. On the policy side, a ten-line truncation rule routed through the same interface lifts agentic solve rate by +14 .3 pp on debug-gym, because shorter contexts let the model attend to the few turns that actually mat-ter. The mechanism is open; the policy space it enables is the agenda. 

1 Introduction 

Agentic LLMs create two distinct cache problems: reuse across natural position shifts, and policy-driven mutation. Prior work addresses the first; this paper addresses the second. KV cache management research has been orga-nized around one workload assumption: prompts arrive once and the cache grows monotonically. The major optimizations in this lineage (prefix caching (Kwon et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024), streaming attention (Xiao et al., 2024), attention-based eviction (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2024)) are correct be-cause the conversation only moves forward: once a token has been prefilled, neither its content nor its position will ever change. The agentic era (Yao et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) breaks this mono-tonicity assumption in two distinct ways, each pro-ducing a different cache-management problem. 

Problem 1: position-invariant reuse. A multi-turn coding or web-research agent re-renders its conversation history every turn. As new tool out-puts and retrieved documents land, previously-seen content shifts to new absolute positions. Exact-prefix matching cannot recover these shifted-but-identical chunks, even though their KV would be reusable if the cache were addressed by content rather than position. Recent MLA-native work (Ma et al., 2026) attempted to solve this reuse problem. 

Problem 2: policy-driven mutation. Distinct from reuse, an agent or policy may need to direct the serving system to actively remove or replace cached content: clear a failed tool call’s output, summarize a stale investigation block to a stub, truncate aged tool outputs to free working memory. 1

These are deliberate edits, not natural shifts. To-day, agentic harnesses handle them by editing the message list and re-prefilling, because no primitive exists for policy-issued cache editing. The cost is the full prefix-recomputation that prefix caching was meant to amortize, and even small per-request invariant breaks can collapse throughput in produc-tion (Cole, 2026). 

Our solution. We introduce Leyline, a serving-side primitive for policy-driven cache editing. The interface is a declarative (span , replacement) di-rective in which the policy describes what to edit, while the kernel applies the edit in place via a closed-form δ-rotation. The intuition is one line: when a splice changes downstream absolute posi-tions by ∆, every cached Kpe value at those posi-tions can be rotated by ∆ to restore attention-math correctness, without re-prefilling anything that did not actually change.

Read together: the mechanism enables the policy class without prefill recompute cost, and the policy class delivers the deployment-cell win. Ex-tending the kernel to GQA appears feasible via a boundary-recomputation pass (Hu et al., 2025) and is sketched in Section 6.

Table 1 positions representative systems against the two sub-problems plus three properties any agentic-era cache primitive needs: correctness after edit, cache-reuse after edit, and turn-granular operation. The first row trades cache reuse to get correct-ness on mutation. The second row addresses neither sub-problem. The third (LMCache) is the production storage and movement layer for vLLM and SGLang (Liu et al., 2025) but oper-ates below the semantic-edit level. The fourth and fifth (CacheBlend, EPIC) are the MHA-side analogs to Irminsul’s MLA-side δ-rotation: both address non-prefix reuse on MHA/GQA via selec-tive recomputation rather than positional correction. CacheBlend dynamically picks high-deviation to-kens layer-by-layer (Yao et al., 2024); EPIC stat-ically picks each chunk’s first k tokens, moti-vated by the attention-sink effect at chunk bound-aries (Hu et al., 2025). The sixth trades correctness for speed. The seventh (Irminsul) handles the MLA reuse half; the eighth (Leyline) contributes the mu-tation half, with CacheBlend/EPIC-style boundary recomputation as the natural mechanism-layer ex-tension we envision for GQA (Section 6). 

Layered relationship to LMCache. LMCache and Leyline operate at different layers and com-pose. LMCache’s verbs (Pin, Clear, Move, Com-press, Lookup) act on KV blocks in physical stor-age tiers (Liu et al., 2025); Leyline’s directive acts on semantic spans of the prompt. The two meet in the middle of one pipeline: a directive “replace this stale tool-output span with a stub” decomposes into block-level operations (free, allocate, Kpe ro-tate, re-index) that LMCache schedules across tiers. Read end-to-end, this is agent intent → semantic di-rective ( Leyline ) → block operations (LMCache) 

→ physical tier movement. Leyline supplies what LMCache does not: position-encoding correctness across a semantic edit, and a span-level interface for the policy to address. Adjacent work on tensor-encoded KV transfer (Liu et al., 2024a) and 2-bit KV quantization (Liu et al., 2024b) is orthogonal to position correctness.

3 Leyline: a directive abstraction for policy-driven cache editing 

Leyline is a serving-side abstraction layer between the agent’s policy and the KV cache. The con-tributions of this section are three: a declarative directive interface (Section 3.1) that decouples pol-icy signal from kernel mechanism, a serving-stack integration that preserves the radix prefix across edits (Section 3.3), and a thin Python policy inter-face that any span-emitting policy can drive (Sec-tion 3.4). The kernel-level correctness mechanism, the δ-rotation rule that re-anchors cached Kpe val-ues after a position shift, is recapped in Section 3.2. Leyline is what turns that rule into a usable primi-tive for explicit policy-driven editing. Three properties from Section 2 fix the suc-cess criteria, and no existing system meets all three at once. First, signal-agnosticism : the prim-itive must accept anything expressible as a set of spans, without privileging where the signal came from (Kariyappa and Suh, 2026; Xu et al., 2026), so today’s detector does not constrain to-morrow’s. Second, positional replay-equivalent correctness : after the edit, downstream attention math must be well-formed at the new positions (Section 3.1 makes the contract precise). Third, 

cache-friendliness : the edit must localize to the spliced span without invalidating the radix prefix, so that the work the prompt edit did not touch sur-vives the edit. 

3.1 The directive: (span , replacement) 

A directive is a 4-tuple D = ( sstart , s end , R, m ),where [sstart , s end ) is the token-index range in the original rendered prompt being replaced, R is the replacement token sequence (often a short stub, e.g. 

[evicted: 3 older tool turns] ), and m ∈ {AMORTIZE , FORGET } selects the semantic mode (we write (span , replacement) in running text when m is the default AMORTIZE ). Multiple non-overlapping directives may be applied per turn. The AMORTIZE -mode contract is positional , not informational : after applying 

D, the cache produces outputs equivalent to one built from the original prompt with downstream positions reindexed by ∆ = |R| − (send − sstart ).Concretely, Kpe is rotated to the new positions, while Knope and V are preserved (they were computed under attention to the original chunk during prefill, and that attention is exactly what we want to keep). Downstream behavior thus reflects the cache’s persistent attention to the original chunk, not the stub. This is deliberately weaker than “equivalent to re-prefill of the substituted prompt”, because re-prefill is precisely the cost the directive amortizes. Section 4 reports empirical agreement against both the full-context baseline (the contract) and the re-prefill path (the stricter contract AMORTIZE does not promise), so the 4reader can see what each path actually predicts. 

The FORGET mode. Some policies need true content forgetting: redaction of sensitive content, retention-mandated deletion, or correction of an up-stream tool error whose continued influence would mislead the agent. These declare m = FORGET ,and the serving stack routes the edit to a standard prefix-trimmed re-prefill, the regime production stacks already implement. The directive layer’s ad-dition is the declared intent: the policy says which guarantee it needs, much as a database client de-clares isolation level rather than relying on serving-side defaults. The empirical validation in this paper is for the AMORTIZE path, which carries the engi-neering novelty; FORGET adds no new kernel work and is included in the contract specifically to head off the “hidden cache influence” failure mode the abstraction would otherwise enable. Governance patterns built on the mode distinction are detailed in App. O.

The directive interface is signal-agnostic. Any-thing producing (sstart , s end , R ) tuples drives the cache; the kernel does not inspect the rationale. This is property (i)’s policy/mechanism decoupling in concrete form, distinct from Irminsul’s content-hash-triggered reuse: a policy that says “drop this aged tool output” is an explicit edit, not a hash hit, and needs an agent-facing invocation path portable across serving stacks. 3.3 Serving-stack integration 

Each directive triggers three steps inside the serv-ing stack (Figure 2), each handling a distinct region of the prompt. (1) For tokens before sstart , the radix cache matches the prompt as usual; the unedited prefix survives the edit, and this is the source of Leyline’s cache-reuse property. (2) For the replace-ment itself, tokens R are prefilled fresh into newly allocated slots at positions [sstart , s start + |R|). (3) For tokens after the edit, cached Kpe for slots orig-inally at i ≥ send is rotated by ∆ (Equation (1)) and reindexed to i + ∆ . Only the third step touches cached state, and it does so with a single matmul per slot. The mechanism is implemented in two paths that share one rotation kernel. The rotation kernel itself 

is a single in-place operation on the Kpe band of a transformers DynamicCache ; we verify against 5DSv2-Lite’s native rotary_emb that the kernel is bit-exact for a single rotation, and this same kernel is the source of truth for all empirical claims below.

3.4 The policy interface 

The directive abstraction admits any policy that maps conversation state to a directive list. We expose a thin Python abstraction with a single 

Policy.transform(messages, turn_ idx) → messages method; Leyline computes the diff against the previous turn and applies it through the kernel mechanism of Section 3.3. A policy can be a turn-counter, a hand rule, a content-pattern matcher, or any function over conversation state. 

4 Demonstrating correctness for the mutation operation 

Prior work establishes the δ-rotation as correct by construction for position shifts in the reuse case (Ma et al., 2026); the directive-driven splice must produce the same guarantee under arbitrary edit patterns. The load-bearing test is the con-structed single-prompt microbenchmark of Sec-tion 4.1. Its design is the point: the elided content is chosen so that the full-context and re-prefill refer-ences must disagree on the next token, making the contract’s distinguishing prediction (Leyline tracks full-context, not re-prefill) directly observable on the first decoded token. The remaining empirical material adds workload coverage: a 12-step policy-derived trajectory replayed across four MLA ar-chitectures, a randomized synthetic edit-suite over arbitrary span positions and signed ∆, kernel-level chained-rotation drift, and 50-step long-horizon trajectories. These tests live in the appendix; Sec-tion 4.2 summarizes the headline numbers. 

4.1 Single-prompt replay equivalence 

This subsection carries the central correctness claim. The setup is a constructed microbench-mark where the references are guaranteed to dis-6agree, so the contract’s distinguishing prediction (Section 3.1: Leyline tracks full, not re-prefill) is testable by construction. The appendix coverage adds architectural and workload breadth but inher-its its interpretive weight from here. 

Setup. A prompt asks the model to recall a calcu-lation buried mid-prompt. The chunk is the literal calculation 25 + 9 = 34 (57 tokens), wrapped by 5 pre-tokens and 9 post-tokens, with the answer requested at the post-decode position. A directive replaces the calculation chunk with the 11-token stub [truncated] (∆= − 46 ). Three paths are compared: full-context (original prompt prefilled honestly), re-prefill (stub-substituted prompt pre-filled honestly), and Leyline (original prompt pre-filled, then directive applied via δ-rotation). 

Result. The full-context path predicts ’34’ (the correct answer); re-prefill predicts ’0’ (the model lost the calculation when the stub replaced it); Ley-line predicts ’34’ (it tracks the full-context cache). This is the positional contract from Section 3.1 in action. Leyline preserves the downstream slots’ 

Knope and V , which were computed under atten-tion to the original chunk, so the cache continues to drive the model toward the chunk-informed output. Re-prefill instead rebuilds those slots’ Knope and 

V against the stub, losing the calculation’s effect on downstream attention. The fact that Leyline tracks full-context, not re-prefill, is therefore the contract, not a bug. A kernel that tracked re-prefill would have to recompute all downstream K/V; that is exactly the prefill cost the directive is designed to avoid. A 4×3×2 ablation (stub modes × trajectories 

× models) confirms the rotation, not stub text, is load-bearing (Appendix M). All decodes here are greedy (argmax, no sampling); model weights are bf16 under stock HuggingFace forward passes; the three paths share identical model and tokenizer state, so observed differences arise only from the cache state at the prefill-to-decode boundary. 

bf16 K-storage precision floor. Independent of rotation precision, the bf16 KV pool itself imposes a structural floor on per-K-entry accuracy. Sweep-ing the rotation kernel at source positions up to 

8836 and |∆| up to 6794 on DSv2-Lite, we mea-sure a uniform ∼1–3% per-entry error from bf16 storage, independent of ∆, vs fp32 where the same kernel returns < 10 −3 error at every tested (p, ∆) .This floor causes occasional argmax flips: on a 50-step long-context agentic replay (App. B), Leyline matches a full-prefill reference on 48 /50 first-token outputs. The mismatches occur on prompts whose top-1/top-2 logit gap is below the bf16 floor; the failure direction is not biased (the same workload’s no-splice path occasionally lands on a different argmax for the same reason). We default AKASHA_ PIC_ROTATION_FP32=1 so the rotation com-putation runs in fp32 (matching the model’s own attention-forward precision policy) even when the KV pool stores bf16; this removes the rotation’s contribution to the floor but does not eliminate the storage’s contribution. The honest claim is bit-faithfulness within bf16 precision: outputs are byte-identical to a full prefill only on prompts with logit-margin above the noise floor. The full per-position error table and the fp32-mitigation decomposition are in App. Q.

5 Deployment-cell evidence at the policy layer 

The directive interface’s value depends on a mea-surable policy-layer signal: a ∼10-line truncation policy routed through the interface should improve agentic task behavior over a no-truncation baseline. The mechanism we expect is straightforward. As a debugging trajectory grows, most of the older tool output is stale; dropping it frees the model’s attention to focus on the small slice that still mat-ters. Across 8 debug-gym (Yuan et al., 2025) mini_nightmare tasks × 4 seeds on JoyAI-LLM Flash under the standard re-prefill arm (32 base-line + 33 treatment trials), the truncation treatment lifts solve rate from 10 /32 = 31 .2% (baseline) to 

15 /33 = 45 .5% (treatment), a +14 .3 pp absolute gain (Table 2). The asymmetry in N reflects one extra counter trial in the treatment arm; the paired analysis below restricts to the symmetric subset. A ninth task, pandas_dataframe, was excluded be-cause it requires fetching an external dataset over the network and our compute nodes have no out-bound internet access; both policies failed equally on every pandas_dataframe trial we ran, so the ex-clusion is environmental rather than policy-related. Across the 31 paired (task, seed) cells where both arms ran with reliable provenance, both policies agree on 17 outcomes; of the 14 cells where they disagree, the treatment wins 9 and the baseline wins 5 ( 1.8× asymmetry). Where the gain concentrates is consistent with the hypothesis: the two tasks with substantial trajectory length, shopping_cart (0% → 75% ) and sum_tree ( 25% → 50% ), do all the work. On tasks that are either too short to trigger truncation or hard enough that neither pol-icy solves them under the 50-step cap, the curves overlap. These are properties of the truncation policy 

routed through standard message-edit semantics; they do not isolate any contribution from Leyline’s splice kernel, whose correctness is established in-dependently by Section 4. This is the policy leg of the two-leg validation.

6 Generalizing beyond MLA: an invitation 

We validate on MLA because it admits the clean-est rotation correction: positions are isolated to a 64-dim RoPE-rotated slice Kpe , leaving the bulk of K position-free (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024a; Ma et al., 2026). The four largest agent-trained open-weight models we evaluated are all MLA (DeepSeek-V2-Lite, JoyAI-LLM Flash, GLM-4.7-Flash, Moonlight-16B-A3B), so the cost of swap-ping architecture for our purposes was zero. Above the kernel, every layer of Leyline is architecture-agnostic by construction: the directive interface, the radix-cache integration, and the policy inter-face depend only on token spans, not on how posi-tions are encoded. Only step (3) of Section 3.3, the rotation kernel itself, is MLA-specific. 

Extension to GQA and MHA. RoPE’s unitary-rotation algebra transfers to GQA and MHA (Su et al., 2024); the complication is that they fuse position throughout the full K tensor rather than isolating it to a slice, so the rotation must act across all dK dimensions per token. Algebra-level and live-model validation are in App. H: the kernel runs end-to-end on Llama-3.1-Minitron-4B and Qwen3-4B without numeric issues, but the model-side ill-formed-cache sensitivity we documented on MLA manifests more strongly because rotation acts on dimensions that also carry content. The boundary-recomputation pass of concurrent reuse-side work (Yao et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2025) is a candidate kernel-layer addition without changing the directive; non-rotary encodings (ALiBi, NoPE, learned absolute) are future work. 

Forward compatibility. The directive abstrac-tion is mechanism-agnostic; the rotation ker-nel is not. Within-MLA architecture evolution (e.g. trained sparsity) inherits splice + δ-rotation directly; out-of-MLA evolution (e.g. sequence-compressed KV) breaks the rotation kernel but the span-level directive remains well-typed. Ap-pendix N develops the two concrete DeepSeek cases (V3.2-Exp (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025), V4 (DeepSeek-AI, 2026)). 

7 Discussion 

The serving-stack interface boundary moves. 

Pre-agentic serving systems had a single inter-face to the agent layer: the prompt. Post-agentic systems need a second one, a cache-edit chan-nel , which Leyline’s directive defines concretely. The interface decouples policy (“what should be in cache?”) from mechanism (“how to splice the cache to match without re-prefill”), so policies can emit (span , replacement) without knowing MLA, RoPE, or radix internals. 

Composition with concurrent and adja-cent work. Signal-side detectors (Side-Quest (Kariyappa and Suh, 2026), LoopGuard (Xu et al., 2026)) and active-RAG retrieval patterns (FLARE (Jiang et al., 2023), Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024), CacheBlend (Yao et al., 2024), EPIC (Hu et al., 2025)) compose naturally with Leyline’s directive interface: any signal expressible as a span-set drives a directive without retraining the kernel or the detector. Furthermore, the recom-putation mechanisms of CacheBlend (dynamic 10 high-deviation token selection) and EPIC (static chunk-boundary recomputation) plug in at the kernel layer beneath the directive, providing the GQA/MHA path identified in Section 6. See Appendix K for the detailed mapping. 

Safety as an API contract. Locating the mode declaration ( AMORTIZE vs FORGET ) at the agent layer rather than the serving stack mirrors how database clients declare isolation level: it moves the choice between in-place amortization and se-mantic forgetting from hidden serving-stack default to a declared, auditable per-edit contract. Concrete governance patterns and the concurrency/tenant-isolation discussion are in App. O. 

Beyond eviction: a programmable memory primitive. The splice verb validated here is one of several the directive layer naturally hosts (App. J). DECAY declares an aging-out window for tool output the next reasoning step is unlikely to revisit (for example, the read stage’s output once 

analyze has consumed it), freeing GPU capacity proactively rather than waiting for end-of-request eviction. PIN declares hard retention for context the agent knows it will revisit, converting specula-tive tool-output prefetch (Nichols et al., 2025) from a probabilistic guess into a declared guarantee; it composes with SHARE to keep a reference code-base reusable across multiple refactoring agents on the same serving stack. SCRATCH carves attention-mask isolation for reasoning that participates in the current step but is reclaimed before the next. A common pattern runs through these verbs: the agent has earlier and more reliable knowledge of context fate than any system-side predictor can in-fer, and Leyline externalizes that knowledge as a semantic-edit layer above the storage layer. The effect is to turn KV cache management from a re-active eviction problem, where the system guesses, into a cooperative scheduling one, where the agent declares and the storage layer executes. 

8 Conclusion 

Agentic LLM serving has two cache sub-problems (Section 2): reuse across position shifts (Irmin-sul (Ma et al., 2026)) and policy-driven mutation (this paper). Leyline addresses mutation through the (span , replacement) directive, a declarative span-level edit interface composed over a closed-form δ-rotation kernel. The kernel preserves down-stream attention against the original chunk while reindexing positions, so the directive can be ap-plied in place without re-prefilling work the edit never touched. The directive abstraction itself is architecture-agnostic, and only the rotation ker-nel is MLA-clean, with the natural extension to GQA/MHA sketched in Section 6. The longer arc is broader than eviction. Once the agent can declare 

what should be in cache , the KV cache becomes a programmable memory layer rather than a system-side guess (Section 7). This paper validates one verb in that layer, splice; the vocabulary the direc-tive interface admits is the agenda the abstraction opens. 

Limitations 

Architecture coverage. The kernel-side rotation is MLA-specific in its implementation (one matmul on the 64-dim Kpe slice). The directive abstrac-tion generalizes, and the rotation algebra transfers to GQA (Ainslie et al., 2023) and MHA at full 

dK width (Section 6, App. H). On live GQA mod-els the rotation acts on dimensions that also carry content, so the same model-side sensitivity to ill-formed cache state observed on MLA manifests more strongly (App. H); a boundary-token recom-putation pass (Hu et al., 2025) as a kernel-layer addition is the natural next step, leaving the direc-tive layer unaffected. 

Numerics. The δ-rotation drift bound ( 4.7×10 −3

rel-L2 per rotation in bf16) was established in prior work (Ma et al., 2026); RoPE’s unitary-rotation closure R(a)R(b) = R(a + b) makes chained ap-plications mathematically equivalent to a single rotation by the sum. Empirically (Appendix F, Ta-ble 6) chained bf16 rotations accumulate drift sub-linearly: 100 chained rotations show 2.6×10 −2 rel-L2 against a fresh-RoPE-at-target reference, 5.5×

the per-rotation bound for a 100 × increase in rota-tion count. Deployments where the same physical slot is re-rotated thousands of times per session would benefit from periodic re-prefill; we have not measured drift in that extreme regime. 

Position-arbitrariness sensitivity. JoyAI and GLM exhibit high model-side sensitivity to position-arbitrary (mid-message, mid-template-tag) splices in the randomized edit-suite (Section E); the policy-derived runs splice at chat-message bound-aries and the same kernel produces clean tool-selection agreement on those models. We read this as model-side sensitivity to ill-formed mid-11 template splices, not a kernel correctness find-ing. Characterizing the gradient between boundary-aligned and arbitrary splices per model is follow-up work. 

Workload coverage. The deployment (Sec-tion 5) covers 8 debug-gym mini_nightmare tasks 

× 4 seeds × 2 policies at a single truncation threshold on one model. We do not characterize how the gains depend jointly on policy aggressive-ness across thresholds, benchmark family (SWE-bench (Jimenez et al., 2023), web-research, longer-horizon workloads), model capability, trajectory-divergence behavior, or eviction aggressiveness. Mapping the surface is deferred to follow-up work, and the mechanism contribution does not depend on which cell wins. 

Composed mechanism × policy ablation. The mechanism leg of the validation runs on synthetic prompts (Section B); the policy leg runs on debug-gym under the standard re-prefill arm (Section 5). The composed ablation that would attribute the deployment win between the two contributions, namely running the splice arm against the same truncation policy on the same debug-gym tasks, is the natural next step. Exercising the splice path through the agent’s per-turn message-edit flow rather than through the controlled microbench of Section B is engineering on the agent–serving seam, not a Leyline-design gap. Section 4 predicts the splice arm would preserve original-chunk con-text that the re-prefill arm loses, and we expect this to manifest as a different (likely smaller) per-step context-cleanup magnitude under splice than under re-prefill. 

Empirical-coverage scope. Appendices E, F, and G cover the corner cases we identified as load-bearing: varied span sizes (8–200 tokens), 1–2 non-overlapping edits per turn, positive and nega-tive ∆, two stub-content modes, chained rotations up to N =100 on the same logical slot, and 50-step trajectories with up to 8 edits per turn. The ran-domized suite uses a single base prompt (counter step 4) per model and 40 trials per cell; widening to multiple base prompts would tighten confidence intervals but is unlikely to change the qualitative picture. The contract’s full-vs-rp prediction is most cleanly testable on Moonlight (where the two refer-ences diverge with non-trivial frequency); a broader suite spanning more diverging-reference-frequent models is follow-up work. 

Determinism controls. All replays in Section 4 use greedy decoding (argmax, no sampling, no temperature) over bf16 model weights loaded via stock HuggingFace forward passes (no fused cus-tom kernels, no cudnn-deterministic flag). The full/re-prefill/Leyline paths share identical model state and tokenizer state on each step. We have not measured whether residual bf16 nondetermin-ism (matmul-reduction ordering on H100 tensor cores) explains any portion of the observed per-step disagreements; the magnitude of cross-path diver-gence we observe (mean common-prefix lengths far from 128 on GLM-4.7-Flash) far exceeds plau-sible bf16 reduction noise, so we attribute the dis-agreements primarily to cached-KV sensitivity, not floating-point reordering. 

bf16 K-storage precision floor. Distinct from matmul-reduction nondeterminism, the bf16 KV pool imposes a structural per-K-entry precision floor of ∼1–3% (uniform across rotation positions; measured in App. Q). Argmax flips happen on the small fraction of prompts ( ∼4% on our long-context replay) whose top-1 vs top-2 logit gap is smaller than that noise. The current mitiga-tion ( AKASHA_PIC_ROTATION_FP32=1 , de-fault on) removes the rotation computation ’s contri-bution by running the cos/sin multiply in fp32; the rotation storage (the bf16 KV pool itself) remains the floor. Workloads that require byte-identical out-puts to a full prefill on every prompt would need either higher-precision K storage or an argmax-margin gate before committing a splice. Workloads that tolerate sampling-noise-scale token differences (chat, long agentic loops where individual token choice is not load-bearing) are unaffected. 

Cold-start coverage and warm-start scope. 

The manifest warm-start mechanism (Section 5, App. S) bypasses the within-batch peer-discovery race only for content the manifest covers, and we have demonstrated its activation in the nar-row 2-tenant C=4 configuration. Tenant-count sweep ( {3, 4, 8} tenants at C=4 ) and concurrency extension ( C=8 at 2 tenants with warm-start) all reach cand_total =0 even though the reg-istry shows 15 –35 unique observed hashes (jobs 

473946 –473952 , App. U). The candidate filter re-jects the warmup-time inserts once the live work-load’s first batch reaches match_prefix , con-sistent with the warmup request’s pool slots having been freed by then. The directive contract (Sec-tion 4) is independent of this filter behavior; closing 12 it is concrete engineering (separate warmup pro-cess, or persistent fetchable representation in the registry) and not on the critical path of the design. 

KV-pool occupancy is not the figure of merit. 

The savings reported in Section 5 are re-prefill compute and pool writes avoided, not concur-rent allocator memory. Role B inserts spliced chunks into the radix trie by reference, so the spliced slots remain trie-pinned and the alloca-tor’s peak available_size() does not drop. The 4-tenant measurement (App. U) shows splice-ON and splice-OFF peaks identical to the slot (97 .5 MB), and the 2-tenant ON + warm-start peak is +12 MB above OFF (from the four warm-up-preloaded articles staying pinned). The matched-configuration tenant slope at concurrency C=4 is 

1.82 × (splice OFF, 2-tenant → 4-tenant) vs 1.71 ×

(splice ON + warm-start, same range; the 4-tenant arm of that pair produces no spliced chunks, so the slope reduction beyond the splice-OFF baseline is from warm-start pinning rather than splice activity at 4-tenant). Read together, the splice’s chunk-level overlap detection produces a measurable but modest tenant-scaling-slope reduction (the linear projection is 2.0×). Deployments whose mem-ory constraint is peak allocator pressure cannot rely on the splice to drop their watermark; deploy-ments whose constraint is prefill compute under high cross-tenant overlap will see the savings as re-prefill ms not spent. 

Logit-level vs token-level equality. The head-line metrics in Tables 4 and 5 are token-level (argmax agreement, common-prefix length, parsed tool-name agreement); Appendix I additionally re-ports per-step logit-level ℓ2 and KL distances be-tween Leyline and the two reference paths on the counter trajectory for all four models. The pic-ture is consistent with argmax: on edit steps where argmax matches, the per-step KL between Leyline and the matched reference is below 0.5 nats; the bulk distribution can still differ on dimensions far from the top, but the rank-1 token is preserved. A logit-level acceptance test for sampling regimes (rather than greedy) would tighten the empirical contract further and is a natural extension. 

Open corner cases for pin-style directives. Adeployed PIN-style directive must answer two engineering questions raised in the vLLM RFC thread (vLLM Project contributors, 2024): resource contention under user-issued pins, and cross-tenant prefix-cache leakage. The directive abstraction is correct independently of either answer (it splices what the policy says to splice); both are deploy-side policy work.

C Kernel specification: tensors and composition 

Step-by-step tensor effect of one directive. Adirective’s kernel effect on the cache is fully speci-fied by four operations on three regions of the cache layout. (a) The evicted span’s slots [sstart , s end )

are freed : Kpe , Knope , and V at those positions are released back to the KV-pool allocator. (b) The replacement R is freshly prefilled into newly allo-cated slots at positions [sstart , s start +|R|), comput-ing K and V for these tokens against the cache pre-fix up to sstart via a normal forward pass. (c) Down-stream slots originally at positions i ≥ send have 

only their Kpe rotated by ∆ (Equation (1)) and are re-keyed in the radix structure to occupy logical positions i + ∆ ; their Knope and V are untouched. (d) No Q tensor is cached for the decode path (Q is recomputed each step from current hidden states), so no Q reindexing is required. Sink tokens (the first N tokens for streaming-attention-style sinks) are unaffected provided sstart > N ; deployments that violate this can either widen sstart or treat the sink prefix as pinned. We rule out layernorm and attention-sink interactions as confounders by val-idating bit-identity in the no-edit case (Section 4) and by the closed-form rotation-algebra argument in Section 3.2. 

Multiple directives, overlapping spans, signed 

∆. A turn may carry multiple directives. We re-quire non-overlapping spans at the directive layer (an overlapping submission is rejected at apply -time; merging two adjacent removals is the policy’s responsibility, not the kernel’s). Within one turn the kernel processes directives left-to-right with the running position shift carried over: a direc-tive at sstart ,2 applied after one with shift ∆1 sees the downstream slots at their already-shifted posi-tions i + ∆ 1, and the rotation algebra closes under composition ( R(∆ 1)R(∆ 2) = R(∆ 1 + ∆ 2), so re-rotating an already-rotated slot is well-defined).

J Programmable memory verbs beyond splice 

This appendix elaborates the four verbs sketched in Section 7. The directive abstraction is the same; each verb names a distinct semantic intent the agent can declare and the kernel can execute. Experimen-tal validation of each is future work. 

Lifecycle hints: DECAY for tool-call chains. 

Multi-step agent workflows have well-defined util-ity horizons. In a read → analyze → write 

pipeline, the KV entries for file contents retrieved during read are no longer needed once analyze 

completes. A semantic DECAY directive issued at the boundary of analyze tells the storage layer to deprioritize these entries proactively (e.g., com-press and migrate to CPU), freeing GPU capac-ity for write without waiting for end-of-request eviction. The agent knows the phase boundary; no system-side predictor does.

Hard retention: PIN for deterministic prefetch. 

Speculative tool-execution work (Nichols et al., 2025) prefetches predicted future tool calls dur-ing generation, reducing end-to-end latency but wasting work on mispredictions. A semantic PIN 

directive provides a complementary mechanism: when an agent explicitly declares “the span I am about to emit will be needed in the next turn,” the storage layer can treat this as a hard retention guar-antee rather than a probabilistic estimate, convert-ing speculation into deterministic prefetch. The block-level Pin in LMCache is the storage-side enforcement; the semantic PIN is the agent-side declaration that produces it. Industry deployments are converging on related designs: analysis of Claude Code’s internals describes a speculative-execution subsystem that pre-runs predicted com-mands in an isolated sandbox before user confirma-tion (Spano, 2026), indicating that agent–serving co-design around execution prediction is already a practical concern. 

Attention-mask isolation: SCRATCH . A natural extension is scratchpad KV — context that par-ticipates in attention during a reasoning step but is excluded from the output stream and reclaimed immediately on span completion. The mask-level mechanism is not in this paper; the interface space that admits it is.

K Composition with concurrent and adjacent work 

Concurrent signal-side work. The signal-side contributions of SideQuest (Kariyappa and Suh, 2026) (learned semantic-utility detector) and Loop-Guard (Xu et al., 2026) (decoder-side degeneration detection) compose naturally with Leyline: any signal expressible as a span-set to evict can drive a Leyline directive without retraining the kernel or the signal detector. The right division of labor in the agentic-LLM stack is signal source in one paper, mechanism abstraction in another. 

Active-RAG. FLARE (Jiang et al., 2023) and Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024) promise uncertainty-triggered retrieval mid-generation, but in produc-tion stumble on two coupled obstacles: calibration breakdown in RLHF-trained models, which makes the uncertainty trigger unreliable; and position-encoding invalidation when retrieved content is spliced into a live cache, which forces a re-prefill on every injection. CacheBlend (Yao et al., 2024) addresses the second obstacle on MHA via selec-tive cross-attention recomputation; for MLA, the position-invariant cKV channel plus the δ-rotation of Ma et al. (2026) provides a structurally cheaper resolution. Leyline’s directive interface makes the first obstacle tractable by separating the uncertainty oracle (an external policy that decides when to re-trieve and what to inject) from the cache-edit mech-anism (the directive that splices without re-prefill). The result is a clean division of labor across the stack: an external oracle supplies policy; the direc-tive supplies the semantic edit; the storage layer (LMCache (Liu et al., 2025), where deployed) han-dles the resulting block-level movement across the GPU/CPU/storage hierarchy. 

Scope notes. Two non-claim items from the main text bear restating. First, we do not develop the side observation that Leyline preserves more con-text than the rendered prompt suggests; that de-pendence is workload-specific and deserves sep-arate empirical treatment. Second, variation we observe across models is in agent behavior under Leyline-managed cache (signal-policy-workload-dependent), not in whether the directive-driven ro-tation works .

L vLLM framework RFC threads 

The block-level lifecycle half of the agentic cache problem is now consolidated in production. LM-Cache (Liu et al., 2025) ships an enterprise-grade control API with Pin, Clear, Move, Compress, and Lookup operating on cache blocks in physical storage tiers, integrated with vLLM and SGLang. vLLM’s internal KVEvents mechanism (con-sumed by the NVIDIA Dynamo router) covers the 24 introspection side. The semantic-edit half has not. Four re-cent vLLM proposals each request a slice of cache control at the layer above the storage backend: [RFC] Pinned Caching (vLLM Project contributors, 2024) asks for manual-expiration directives over the radix cache (a higher-level Pin than LMCache’s block-level Pin); 

[RFC] KV-Cache Interoperability API (vLLM Project contributors, 2025b) proposes to expose KVEvents as a public contract for external policy consumers; [RFC] Sparse KV Cache Management (vLLM Project contributors, 2025c) sketches a pluggable frame-work for non-LRU eviction policies; [RFC] Generalized KV Cache Reuse (vLLM Project contributors, 2025a) lifts the prefix-only restriction so non-prefix token subsets can be reused, the necessary precondition for splice. None of these proposals defines a span-level edit operation with the position-encoding correctness story that splicing through the kernel requires; each surfaces a piece of the unsolved layer. The threads on these four RFCs follow a consis-tent pattern. The maintainers engage substantively, raise corner cases (resource contention when GPU memory runs out under user-issued pins, cross-user prefix-cache leakage under content-addressed cache, hole-filling under H2O-style sparse evic-tion), then run out of consensus on the right level of abstraction. The Sparse-KV thread is exem-plary: contributors converge on the diagnosis that the framework needs to decouple logical from phys-ical memory management so that the scheduler does not have to know which eviction or offloading policy is in effect, but the discussion ends without a maintainer-accepted abstraction (vLLM Project contributors, 2025c). The same shape recurs in the Generalized-Reuse follow-up: with no cache-layer primitive available, contributors propose a request-batching workaround — splitting one prompt into three ( tokens 0-9, 0-19, 0-29 ), batching with two as placeholders, discarding the place-holder outputs — to recompute holes outside the cache layer entirely (vLLM Project contributors, 2025a). Leyline’s directive abstraction is the layer this pattern is missing: a single declarative interface in which all four verbs (pin, expose, evict, splice) are well-typed, with one mathematical foundation (the δ-rotation rule) that gives correctness across the vocabulary — and which the kernel applies in place rather than forcing the workaround out to the scheduler layer. The mutation half currently forces deployments into a correctness-versus-reuse tradeoff that Leyline closes.

N Forward compatibility across attention-architecture changes 

The δ-rotation correctness story applies to DeepSeek V2/V3-class MLA, where the trained model expects RoPE-encoded keys at their origi-nal absolute positions and attends densely over the causal range. Two recent DeepSeek releases test the directive abstraction at different points in the attention-architecture design space — one that pre-serves MLA and adds trained sparsity (V3.2-Exp), and one that replaces MLA outright (V4). 

Within-MLA evolution: V3.2-Exp. DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025) retains MLA and adds DeepSeek Sparse Attention (DSA): a learned Lightning Indexer scores each query 25 against MLA’s compressed token representations and the model attends only to the top-k selected keys per query rather than to a dense window. Two properties carry over from the dense MLA baseline. First, causality is preserved : the indexer selects only from the causal range, and the selected posi-tions remain in their natural causal order. Second, RoPE correctness is per-position: each selected key carries its RoPE encoding at its original absolute position. Sparse attention is a trained-in operation, not a license to feed the model K vectors at arbi-trary new positions. What V3.2-Exp changes for the directive ab-straction is the cost of non-splice verbs. PIN - and 

SHARE -style retention (Appendix J) can be imple-mented more efficiently when the kernel is sparse-aware: a content-addressed store of pinned spans composes naturally with top-k selection, and the indexer can be biased to include or exclude partic-ular spans without touching positional encodings. A splice that re-anchors downstream positions still requires the δ-rotation correction or an equivalent, because RoPE correctness applies regardless of which attention pattern — dense or sparse — con-sumes the keys. 

Out-of-MLA evolution: DSv4. DeepSeek-V4 (DeepSeek-AI, 2026) replaces MLA with a Hy-brid Attention Architecture: Compressed Sparse Attention (CSA), which compresses the KV cache along the sequence dimension and applies DSA-style top-k selection, and Heavily Compressed At-tention (HCA), which applies heavier sequence-dimension compression with dense attention. The 

cKV /Kpe /Knope structure of MLA is no longer present; MLA’s per-position RoPE-on-Kpe cor-rectness argument does not transfer to a sequence-compressed representation. Whatever position-correctness story V4 admits (CSA-specific, HCA-specific, or layer-mixed) lives below the directive interface. The directive itself remains well-typed: a span [sstart , s end ) to splice with replacement R is the same input regardless of how the kernel realizes it. Empirical validation on V4-class architectures is out of scope for this paper. For the four MLA configurations validated in Section D — DeepSeek-V2-Lite, JoyAI-LLM Flash (DSv3-derived), GLM-4.7-Flash, Moonlight-16B-A3B — splice + δ-rotation is the correctness mechanism, and V3.2-Exp inherits it with sparse-aware optimizations available for non-splice verbs. V4 is the case that motivates keeping policy and mechanism on different sides of the directive: the rotation kernel is MLA-specific and will not survive the architecture replacement, but the span-level in-terface above it does.

The serving telemetry distinguishes the two modes, so an audit pipeline can answer “which evictions truly erased context vs. amortized it forward” without inspecting the cache. (iii) Reviewer-debugger consistency. A debugger or safety auditor inspecting a conversation transcript needs to know whether displayed-prompt edits are

Open-source release plan. The artifacts de-scribed in this paper — the MLA Kpe rota-tion kernel, the policy-driven directive API in-cluding the AMORTIZE /FORGET mode split, the live SGLang content-hash hook, and the Hug-gingFace transformers replay path used for Sec-tion 4 — will be released under a permissive license on de-anonymization. Subse-quent work — vendor / framework-specific adap-tations, complementary recomputation strategies under the directive layer (Section 6), and signal-side policies (Section K) — should plug in without forking the kernel.

We hypothesize the relevant architectural cues are (a) the degree to which K dimensions co-encode con-tent and position (full in GQA/MHA, partial-K in MLA), (b) the attention-sink strength at the chunk-start position (stronger sinks make the rotated suffix more sensitive to boundary mismatch), and (c) the rope frequency band the rotation crosses for typical 

|∆| in the workload. A lightweight runtime detec-tor — decode the first rotated-suffix token under both rotate-only and rotate+ k-recompute, and se-lect whichever matches the full-context reference at higher confidence — is implementable in the existing kernel and is the natural follow-up. The directive layer is unchanged in either case. 

Concurrency, batching, and tenant isolation. 

The Section 3.3 pipeline is described per-request; production deployments need three additional guar-antees. (i) Per-request transactionality of multi-directive turns. A turn carrying k directives must commit all or none. The kernel applies them left-to-right and the rotation algebra closes under compo-sition ( R(∆ 1)R(∆ 2)= R(∆ 1+∆ 2)), but the radix update should be a single transaction so a par-tial failure rolls back to the pre-turn cache state. This matches existing SGLang scheduler seman-tics; the directive layer adds no new atomicity re-quirement beyond what the underlying radix-cache writeback already provides. (ii) Cross-tenant iso-lation under shared prefixes. When tenants share a prompt prefix in the radix tree, a directive is-sued by tenant A at a position downstream of the shared subtree must not affect tenant B’s view of the cache. Leyline’s implementation routes each directive through the tenant-scoped allocator that already owns the slot range past the shared pre-fix; the rotation operates on the local (per-tenant) copy of Kpe and never touches the shared sub-tree. Concretely the reference cache carries a per-chunk ChunkEntry.tenant_tag field with cross-tenant filter on splice candidate selection; default tenant_tag=None is the shared-pool opt-in (behaviorally identical to the pre-tenancy baseline), and activation is per-request (operator sets req._akasha_tenant_tag from request headers), so per-tenant resource accounting com-poses with the splice mechanism without a kernel-level change. (iii) Pinned segments. The PIN 

verb of Section 7 interacts with the cross-tenant case (does a pinned tenant-A segment block tenant-

B from reclaiming allocator budget?); the vLLM RFC thread on pinned caching identifies this as a deploy-side policy question rather than a kernel correctness question (App. L). The directive layer expresses both the edit and the per-tenant scope; the allocator and the radix layer enforce the isolation.

R Role B: radix-trie pre-population via insert + re-match 

Two integration paths share the rotation kernel. 

Role A (the original) intercepts match_prefix ,returns a patched MatchResult with borrowed slots appended, and tracks the borrowed slots on the request for free at cache_finished_req .

Role B Level 2 goes one layer further: after a suc-cessful splice, it calls tree_cache.insert( InsertParams(key=token_ids[:cur_ prefix_end], value=concat(orig_ indices, dst_slots))) and re-runs the un-wrapped match_prefix (stashed at install time as tree_cache._akasha_orig_match_ prefix ) to obtain a native, longer trie match. The scheduler’s inc_lock_ref then locks the new last node, the slots are owned by the radix trie, and the borrowed-slot orphan-detection retires. Future requests with the same prefix match the spliced range natively via match_prefix , without any Leyline hook at lookup time. V Related-work positioning: position-shifted vs cross-instance reuse 

The Leyline contribution lives at the position-shifted reuse layer: a kernel that rotates cached 

Kpe by ∆ so a single instance can reuse content at shifted positions, plus the directive abstraction that lets a policy drive that rotation. The cross-session sharing verb (Section J) and the warm-start mani-fest (Section S) are the first steps of a complemen-tary layer: cross-process / cross-instance content reuse . The two share content-hash addressing as their handle on identity. The cross-instance KV transfer story (live byte movement between GPU pools across a cluster, with the consumer applying the Leyline-style rotation on top of the transferred bytes) is the natural extension; we treat the mani-fest as the static-disk version of what a live RDMA fetch would do dynamically. The infrastructure for that live fetch is the subject of follow-up work and is out of scope here.

A2 Potential risks. Leyline is a serving-side mechanism for KV-cache editing; it does not al-ter the model weights, training data, or output content. Two deploy-side risks warrant explicit naming, both already surfaced in Limitations and the [RFC] Pinned Caching thread (vLLM Project contributors, 2024): (i) cross-tenant prefix-cache leakage under content-addressed caching admits a prefix-guessing side-channel, which ex-plicit user-keyed partitioning at deploy time closes; (ii) user-issued retention directives in future de-ployments could exhaust KV memory, requiring per-request fail-fast policy. Neither is a property of the directive abstraction itself; both are deploy-side policy work.
